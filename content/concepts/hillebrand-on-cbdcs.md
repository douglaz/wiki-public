# Hillebrand on Central Bank Digital Currencies

> "Central Bank Digital Currencies combine all three intervention types into a unified control mechanism. CBDCs are programmable money enabling comprehensive surveillance and control, not simple digital versions of existing currency. … The currency itself refuses to execute disfavored payments. … Money can be programmed to expire, forcing spending and preventing saving; this implements negative interest rates without the zero lower bound."

Hillebrand's [[the-praxeology-of-privacy|The Praxeology of Privacy]] Ch. 10 applies Rothbard's tripartite intervention typology from [[power-and-market|Power and Market]] Ch. 3 to a CBDC and concludes that the architecture fuses all three categories at once. Autistic intervention is embedded in the protocol — "the transaction simply fails" when the ledger refuses to execute a disfavored payment. Binary intervention is the default state of every account — "the state extracts transaction data directly, and every economic act is reported instantly to the monetary authority" — because the central bank is the counterparty to every balance and "reads its own ledger" without subpoena. Triangular intervention persists on the merchant side — programmable rules can require sellers to verify customer compliance status before transacting. The claim is categorical, not speculative: in Hillebrand's frame the design choices being announced by central banks — holding caps, programmable conditions, automatic expiry — are not implementation accidents but the architecture's intended use.

Two design features carry most of the weight. A **per-resident holding cap** turns ordinary savings into a balance the central bank can refuse to honor beyond a threshold — what Hillebrand calls eliminating the commercial-bank buffer that previously sat between citizens and base money. **Automatic expiry on unspent balances** is the programmable instance of negative interest: "money can be programmed to expire, forcing spending and preventing saving." Both features turn money from a freely held store of value into a conditional permission whose terms the issuer rewrites unilaterally. The "two-tier" framing — commercial banks as customer-facing interfaces over a central-bank ledger — does not weaken the analysis; on Hillebrand's reading "the surveillance and control capabilities remain intact; only the user interface is delegated."
