# Mises on Credit Expansion

> "Sooner or later it must result in a catastrophe. … The alternative is only whether the crisis should come sooner as the result of a voluntary abandonment of further credit expansion, or later as a final and total catastrophe of the currency system involved."

Mises's [[human-action|Human Action]] Ch. XX states the mechanism categorically. An increase in the money supply offered on the loan market lowers the gross market rate of interest "although no change in the rate of original interest has taken place." Entrepreneurs respond to the suppressed rate as if voluntary savings had risen, undertake longer and more capital-intensive projects, and bid up the prices of the capital goods those projects need. The corrective crisis is not an external shock; it is the moment at which the longer projects collide with the underlying time-preference structure that did not, in fact, change. Mises's policy summary above admits only one degree of freedom: when the crisis arrives, not whether.

The frame is mechanical and one-directional: rate suppression by the monetary authority → distorted structure of production → forced liquidation. It does not distinguish whether the central bank cuts the policy rate to "support employment," "stabilize prices," or "ease financial conditions"; the cited reason changes the rhetoric but not the analysis. Interest-rate-sensitive sectors — housing, long-duration capital projects, asset prices — are where the boom shows up first and most visibly, because their demand is most elastic to the cost of credit.
