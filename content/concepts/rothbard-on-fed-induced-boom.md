# Rothbard on Fed-Induced Booms

> "In the purely free and unhampered market, there will be no cluster of errors, since trained entrepreneurs will not all make errors at the same time. The 'boom-bust' cycle is generated by monetary intervention in the market, specifically bank credit expansion … For this expansion distorts the structure of investment and production, causing excessive investment in unsound projects in the capital goods industries."

Rothbard's *America's Great Depression* applies the Mises-Hayek framework to a specific monetary authority and a specific historical episode: the Federal Reserve under Benjamin Strong, the 1920s credit inflation, and the unavoidable 1929 correction. The mechanism Rothbard identifies is general — credit expansion induces a *cluster* of errors precisely because it suppresses the same signal across the entire economy at once — and the policy implication runs both ways. Sustained rate suppression *creates* the boom; the boom's most visible expressions are in interest-rate-sensitive durable sectors (housing, long-duration capital projects, asset prices), where demand is most elastic to the cost of credit. Rothbard's later commentary (4th-edition introduction) names the symmetric collapse: *"The housing market has at last been stopped short by the high mortgage rates."*

A rate cut, in this frame, is neither neutral nor merely "supportive." It is the active monetary intervention that initiates the cluster-of-errors pattern in the first place. The labor-market or price-stability rhetoric used to justify the cut changes the press release; it does not change the analysis. The cited stimulation of housing demand is the predicted near-term effect; the predicted later effect is the corrective downturn whose timing depends on whether expansion continues or stops.
