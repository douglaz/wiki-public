# Rothbard on Price Controls

> "price control creates an artificial shortage of the product, which continues as long as the control is in existence—in fact, becomes ever worse as resources have time to shift to other products."

Rothbard's [[man-economy-and-state|Power and Market]] treatment of a maximum-price control is categorical: an effective ceiling — one set below the market-clearing price — opens a gap between quantity demanded and quantity supplied, and the gap widens over time as production reallocates away from the controlled good. The supply-elasticity rider runs in the same direction: *"the more 'elastic' the supply, i.e., the more resources shift out of production, the more aggravated, ceteris paribus, the shortage will be."* Rental housing is supply-elastic on the relevant margins — units can be withheld, converted, or left unmaintained — so the predicted shortage under control is severe and the predicted supply rebound under repeal is sharp.

The policy implication Rothbard draws is symmetric and rare in standard public-finance writing: *"if the government were really worried about the short supply of certain products, it would go out of its way not to impose maximum price controls upon them."* The argument is descriptive about direction, agnostic about magnitude, and indifferent to which jurisdiction supplies the test case.
