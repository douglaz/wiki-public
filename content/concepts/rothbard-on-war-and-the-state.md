# Rothbard on War and the State

> "In war, State power is pushed to its ultimate, and, under the slogans of 'defense' and 'emergency,' it can impose a tyranny upon the public such as might be openly resisted in time of peace. War thus provides many benefits to a State, and indeed every modern war has brought to the warring peoples a permanent legacy of increased State burdens upon society."

The passage from [[anatomy-of-the-state|Anatomy of the State]] names two analytically distinct effects. The first is rhetorical: *"defense"* and *"emergency"* are operative slogans that lower the political cost of measures the public would resist in peacetime. The second is structural: the institutional capacity created to prosecute the war — taxes, conscription machinery, debt, surveillance apparatus, procurement bureaus — does not decay back to pre-war levels when the immediate conflict ends. *"Every modern war has brought to the warring peoples a permanent legacy of increased State burdens upon society."* [[murray-n-rothbard|Rothbard]] endorses Randolph Bourne's compressed form: *"war is the health of the State."*

The frame matters because [[state-power-and-intervention|State Power and Intervention]] treats intervention as a cumulative process rather than a sequence of independent policy choices. Permanent extraction increments justified by transitory threats are a special case of that frame: the threat-citation supplies political cover for the extraction; the extraction outlasts the threat. The [[political-means-and-economic-means|political-means / economic-means]] distinction classifies the increment as a use of the political means, regardless of the threat's reality.
