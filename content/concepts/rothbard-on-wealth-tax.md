# Rothbard on the Wealth Tax

> "It is clear that the wealth tax levies a heavy penalty on accumulated wealth and that therefore the effect of the tax will be to slash accumulated capital. No quicker route could be found to promote capital consumption and general impoverishment than to penalize the accumulation of capital."

Rothbard's [[power-and-market|Power and Market]] section "A Tax on Individual Wealth" treats the wealth tax as a distinct category, not a variant of the income tax. Two structural features matter. First, *"A tax on individual wealth could not be capitalized, since the tax would not be attached to a property, where it could be discounted by the market. Like an individual income tax, it could not be shifted, although it would have important effects."* The market's usual cushioning mechanism — tax capitalization, which lowers the asset's capital value so that future buyers earn the prevailing rate of return — is unavailable. The full impact lands on the holder. Second, the asset base is the heritage from past saving, not current production: *"Accumulated capital … is our heritage from the past; it is the accumulation of tools and equipment and resources from which our present and future standard of living derive. To tax this capital is to reduce the stock of capital, especially to discourage replacements as well as new accumulations, and to impoverish society in the future."*

Rothbard's directional claim is categorical, not magnitude-specific: the tax slashes accumulated capital, accelerates capital consumption, and lowers the future standard of living. The corollary on incidence is that holders with low current income relative to their stock (retirees, illiquid family-firm owners, founders, capital-intensive landlords) are forced to sell assets to pay the tax — *"Robinson … must pay the tax by selling his assets, thereby reducing his accumulated wealth."* The aggregate effect is the conversion of productive capital into government current consumption, with the conversion direction running irreversibly from saved capital to spent revenue. The argument is descriptive about direction and indifferent to the political rhetoric that surrounds the policy.
