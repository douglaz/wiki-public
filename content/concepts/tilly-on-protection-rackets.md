# Tilly on Protection Rackets

> "If protection rackets represent organised crime at its smoothest, then war making and state making — quintessential protection rackets with the advantage of legitimacy — qualify as our largest examples of organised crime."

The 1985 essay [[war-making-and-state-making-as-organized-crime|War Making and State Making as Organized Crime]] is the canonical source for the protection-racket formulation. [[charles-tilly|Tilly]] immediately qualifies the formulation as an *analogy* — *"Without branding all generals and statesmen as murderers or thieves, I want to urge the value of that analogy"* — and limits the historical scope to "the European experience of the past few centuries." The analytic content is the racketeer test: *"Someone who produces both the danger and, at a price, the shield against it is a racketeer."* The wiki uses this as a descriptive comparator to the [[political-means-and-economic-means|political-means / economic-means]] distinction; Tilly does not infer market anarchism from his account, and that limit is preserved here.

Tilly's four-part model — war making, state making, protection, and extraction — is the historical mechanism the analogy names. Extraction supplies the resources for the other three, *"in the long run … in establishing regular access to capitalists who could supply and arrange credit and in imposing one form of regular taxation or another on the people and activities within their spheres of control."* Increases in war-making capacity therefore appear in the model as increases in the extraction base they require, not as policy choices separable from it.
